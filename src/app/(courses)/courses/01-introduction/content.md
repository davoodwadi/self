## Title Slide 
- Applications of AI in Business: Introduction
- A recap of AI fundamentals for MBA and executive audiences
- Framing the managerial questions that will carry through the rest of the course

## The Strategic Case for AI [quiz]
- AI matters because it changes how firms make decisions, scale expertise, and redesign workflows.
- The executive question is not whether AI exists, but where it creates measurable business advantage.
- In practice, AI shows up through revenue growth, cost reduction, risk control, and faster learning cycles.

## What AI Is, and Is Not [quiz]
- AI refers to systems that perform tasks associated with perception, prediction, language, recommendation, or generation.
- AI is not magic, autonomous wisdom, or a substitute for managerial accountability.
- Most business value comes from pairing statistical systems with clear process design and human judgment.

## The AI Family Tree
- Artificial Intelligence is the broad umbrella that includes machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI.
- Machine learning focuses on learning patterns from data rather than following only hard-coded rules.
- Deep learning is a more specialized approach that becomes especially useful with complex data such as images, speech, and language.
- Generative AI produces new text, images, code, or designs rather than only classifying or predicting.

## Three Business Tasks AI Does Best [quiz]
- Prediction: estimating what is likely to happen next, such as churn, demand, or fraud.
- Classification: assigning items to categories, such as sentiment, risk level, or document type.
- Generation: creating drafts, options, summaries, or designs that accelerate human work.

## The Business Value Chain of AI
- AI creates value when it improves a business decision, a customer interaction, or an operational workflow.
- The technology alone is rarely the advantage; the advantage comes from embedding it into repeatable processes.
- Strong use cases usually connect a model output to a clear action, owner, and measurable outcome.

## Data: The Fuel, the Constraint, the Differentiator
- AI depends on access to relevant, timely, and trustworthy data.
- Poor data quality can make a sophisticated model less useful than a simpler alternative.
- Firms with better data access, cleaner processes, and stronger governance often outperform firms with more ambitious models.

## How AI Systems Learn from Patterns
- AI systems are trained on examples, detect statistical regularities, and then apply those learned patterns to new cases.
- This means performance depends heavily on how closely future conditions resemble the data used for training.
- Models do not understand context the way executives do; they optimize against patterns they have seen.

## Where Human Judgment Still Matters
- Leaders still define the goal, the acceptable tradeoffs, and the consequences of failure.
- Human oversight is most important when decisions affect reputation, fairness, safety, or major capital allocation.
- The strongest operating model is not human versus machine, but human judgment amplified by machine speed.
- Discussion: Which decision in your organization should remain human-led even if an AI system becomes highly accurate, and why?

## The Limits of AI in Real Organizations
- AI can be brittle when customer behavior, market conditions, or internal processes change.
- Models can be wrong for reasons that are not obvious to non-technical users.
- Many failures come from workflow misalignment, weak adoption, or unclear ownership rather than from the algorithm itself.

## The Tradeoff: Accuracy, Explainability, and Control
- High-performing models are not always the easiest models to explain, audit, or govern.
- In some settings, a slightly less accurate but more transparent system is the better managerial choice.
- The right balance depends on regulation, customer trust, operational risk, and the reversibility of mistakes.
- Discussion: When should a business accept lower model accuracy in exchange for stronger explainability and control?

## Bridge to Deep Learning
- Later in the course, deep learning will extend today's fundamentals into settings with images, language, and other complex data.
- The key bridge is simple: deeper models can capture richer patterns, but they also increase cost, opacity, and governance demands.
- Before discussing advanced architectures, executives must understand the business problem the model is meant to solve.

## Bridge to EDII
- AI systems reflect the assumptions, exclusions, and historical biases embedded in their data and design choices.
- This makes equity, diversity, inclusion, and indigeneity central governance issues, not optional ethical add-ons.
- Business leaders need to ask not only whether a model works, but for whom it works and who may be harmed.
- Discussion: If an AI system improves efficiency for most users but systematically excludes a smaller group, how should leadership respond?

## Bridge to Sustainability
- AI can help optimize logistics, forecasting, energy use, and resource allocation.
- At the same time, AI systems consume compute, infrastructure, and energy that carry environmental costs.
- The managerial challenge is to evaluate both the sustainability benefits AI creates and the footprint it imposes.

## Bridge to Product Development
- AI is changing product development from idea generation through prototyping, testing, and iteration.
- The core logic remains the same: value comes from pairing data, models, and human expertise around a real business objective.
- This week establishes the managerial lens needed to judge where augmentation improves innovation and where human design leadership remains essential.

## An Executive Adoption Playbook [quiz]
- Start with a specific business problem, not with a vague ambition to "do AI."
- Choose use cases with accessible data, clear owners, and an outcome that can be measured in business terms.
- Pilot narrowly, learn quickly, and scale only when workflow adoption and governance are in place.

## A Functional Map of AI Opportunities
- Marketing can use AI for segmentation, content support, pricing insight, and demand forecasting.
- Operations can use AI for scheduling, quality monitoring, inventory planning, and exception detection.
- Finance and risk teams can use AI for anomaly detection, forecasting, compliance monitoring, and scenario analysis.
- HR and service teams can use AI for talent support, knowledge retrieval, service triage, and workflow automation.

## Executive Questions Before You Scale
- What decision or workflow is being improved, and who is accountable for the result?
- What data foundation, governance process, and change management effort are required for this use case to succeed?
- What would failure look like in operational, ethical, legal, and reputational terms?

## Conclusion: From Curiosity to Competitive Advantage
- AI becomes strategic when it is connected to business priorities, operating discipline, and responsible leadership.
- The goal of this course is to build managerial fluency, not technical theater.
- With that foundation in place, later weeks can explore advanced methods and sector applications with better judgment.
